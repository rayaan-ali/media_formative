Decades of Promises: Indigenous Land Sovereignty Ignored Despite International Law

Rayaan Ali | March 2026 | Estimated: 10 min read

It took centuries of waiting and being ignored, for decades of active resistance. Land sovereignty is an unresolved issue, the Mohawk waiting since 1761, First Nations waiting for more than two centuries. Indigenous people now speak louder than ever through resistance by the media, protests, documentation to hold the government accountable to the laws described in UNDRIP. So when will sovereignty and rights be recognized on unceded land?

Paula Longboat of Six Nations of Grand River holds the Mohawk Warrior Society Flag at a Wet’suwet’en solidarity rally in Ottawa, February 2020 (Mathew Kupfer / CBC News)

Alexis Odjick speaks to the government on how reconciliation has become lip service, and that the time to speak is now: "We're out here today to show that we're no longer going to be silenced" (Kupfer).

Paula Longboat says "They said, mum, we're not going to have anything if we don't stand up and say something now" (Kupfer), emphasizing how children and our next generation continue to stand for sovereignty of their land.

A pattern centuries in the making

"Algonquin chiefs have petitioned the Crown for land for more than two hundred years. Canada has been on notice for two centuries."

Chief Linda Robinson, Wolf Lake First Nation (APTN News)
A common barrier shown by resistance is that Indigenous land rights and sovereignty are not recognized by the government Throughout the timeline, Indigenous land rights and sovereignty are consistently not recognized by Canada. The Kanesatake Resistance was primarily fueled by the industrial land development of a golf course on disputed land and a non recognition of land throughout centuries. The unrecognized land of Wet’suwet’en was restricted by pipeline construction. The BC provincial government unethically bypassed the Hereditary Chiefs by making an agreement with band councils leading to national protests. The land of the Wolf Lake First Nation petitioned the crown for over 200 years, still left without reserve land or modern treaty. Every resistance pushes back on refusal to recognize Indigenous land rights, sovereignty, and consent. Each resistance pushes towards Indigenous land sovereignty. The Kanesatake resistance is a symbol of inspiration through the endurance of violent oppression. The Wet’suwet’en resistance gathered Canadians to show their solidarity. The Wolf Lake First Nation sent a message to call Canadians for support. Each resistance visibly recognizes the constant perseverance of First Nations to withstand land oppression.

Photo of Waneek Horn-Miller after being stabbed in the chest Waneek Horn-Miller holds her sister on September 26th in 1990 during the Kanesatake Resistance (de Bruin)

Waneek Horn-Miller, a fourteen year old teenager holding her younger sister stabbed by a soldier. Despite 75 Mohawk injured during the standoff, the resistance grew across the country as protests were held in support of the Mohawk. Other First Nations signified solidarity through protests or blockades of roads and Railways. Waneek Horn-Miller didn’t stop, and neither did the resistance. Waneek went on to finish 5th at the Sydney 2000 Olympics as a Native Olympic Athlete in Water polo. The Kanesatake Resistance was crucial in the development of the Royal Commission on Aboriginal People. This commission investigated Indigenous land rights and the need for government accountability. A report was released later in 1996 leading to the conclusion of a restructure “The main conclusion was the need for a complete restructuring of the relationship between Indigenous and non-Indigenous peoples in Canada.” (de Bruin). The same force used against Waneek in 1990 is repeatedly shown in the militarized RCMP raids that led to the arrest of 74 Wet’suwet’en land defenders decades later. A resistance grew in 78 days of battle leading to the recognition of Indigenous land rights as a national issue. Can current resistances continue to fight for the same purposes of sovereignty through national support?

Resistance as visibility

The value of land holds stories, culture, and history allowing Indigenous people to persevere against encroachment. Understanding the resistances and battles the Indigenous people are fighting for allow for Canadians to raise their voices in support of Indigenous land rights. Without resistances like Kanesatake and Wet’suwet’en, many Canadians would never realize that the government has consistently ignored Indigenous land rights and sovereignty for centuries. The silencing of the Wet’suwet’en Hereditary Chiefs through band council agreements under the Indian Act, and the criminalization of land defenders exposed the inequalities to Canadians. This criminalization allows for groups such as Amnesty International to partner with Wet’suwet’en to create a project documenting the criminalization for support in future trials against the provincial governments. Specifically, after the large scale police raids on the Wet’suwet’en land defenders the outcome of 74 people arrested led to national media coverage and documentation. This forced Canadians to confront the reality of what happens on unceded land. Through the acknowledgement of these injustices, national protests such as the Canadian 2020 pipeline protest allowed Canadians to show solidarity.

"Without a reserve, our members are excluded from infrastructure, housing and essential services. Services without land is not a remedy."

Chief Linda Robinson, Wolf Lake First Nation (APTN News)

What needs to change

Holding the government accountable to the laws set in UNDRIP Resistance brings mutual understanding to all Canadians and others as to why Indigenous people continue to fight for their land rights despite the silence faced by the government. To understand the values of what Indigenous people fight for, allow Canadians to recognize and stand together to support Indigenous land sovereignty. Resistance shows the need for the Canadian government to recognize the laws set by UNDRIP. Holding the government accountable to UNDRIP through means such as media, protest, documentation, allows for equal justice for Indigenous people fighting for land rights. UNDRIP requires governments to get free, prior, and informed consent before any development on their territory.

Key terms

Ceded
Land or rights that were surrendered, transferred, or given up
Unceded
Lands and territories that were never formally surrendered or given up
UNDRIP
Set the minimum standards for the survival, dignity, and well-being of indigenous people world wide
Reserves
Land set aside by the government for Indigenous peoples

Timeline of resistance

1761

This year, the Mohawk started pushing for recognition of their rights to land

1990

The Kanesatake resistance took place for 78 days between Mohawk protests, RCMP, Quebec police, and the Canadian Army

2007

On September 13th, UNDRIP was implemented in the General assembly. However, Canada voted against it.

2018

The BC supreme court made it illegal for Wet’suwet’en land defenders to block pipeline construction in their own territory.

2020

National protests erupted to defend the land despite potential legal charges such as arrests

2021

UNDRIP became law in Canada.

2026

Wolf Lake First Nations continue a 2 century land claim battle for their land rights.
